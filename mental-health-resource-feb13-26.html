Stress, anxiety, and fear are gripping our newcomer neighbors faced with increased immigration actions in communities nationwide. Living in a prolonged state of stress puts refugee newcomers back into “survival mode,” and in some cases triggers Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), depression, and even physical health conditions.

See how you can help

Dr. Suzy Ismail, founding director of Cornerstone Marriage & Family Intervention, has witnessed the impact on her newcomer clients firsthand.

She explains that many of the newcomers they serve across the U.S. are experiencing severe retraumatization as a result of intensified immigration enforcement actions, including detainments, ICE activity, challenges around reparole, and the spread of negative and hostile rhetoric in the media.

In this new guide, Dr. Ismail shares simple acts of care and connection that can make a profound difference for families during these challenging times.

Get the guide

“Families, children, and elderly living in the U.S. where they sought safe haven are now terrified of leaving their homes, going to school, picking up food, going to work, or any other type of engagement that would allow them to live an emotionally safe life,” Dr. Ismail said.

As Welcomers, we have the opportunity to be a steady presence. By checking in, offering practical support, and creating spaces for connection, we can help ease concerns, provide stability, and reduce anxiety.

In this guide, you’ll have access to:

  • Practical ways to offer care and connection
  • Ways to start a conversation about emotions and well-being
  • Additional mental health resources from trusted organizations
