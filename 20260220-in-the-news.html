This week, the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) issued a memo stating that refugees who have been in the United States for more than one year and have not yet applied for a green card may be subject to arrest and detention.

Read full update

While there are still open legal questions about how this policy may be enforced, it builds on enforcement tactics used in Minneapolis under Operation PARRIS, where refugees were detained in their communities and transferred out of state while their cases were re-examined.

For families, the fear is not abstract.

Thi Dua Vang, a Hmong refugee who fled religious persecution in Vietnam, was arrested in Minnesota and flown to a detention center in Texas. After nearly two weeks in detention, a judge ordered her release—but she was left alone outside the facility without identification or money and told to find her own way home. Her family drove hours from Minnesota to bring her back. “I was crying hard, and I felt hopeless,” she said. “It traumatized me, and it reminded me of what I went through in Vietnam.”

Stories like Vang’s illustrate what policy shifts can mean in real life: sudden detention, family separation, uncertainty, and renewed trauma for people who have already fled persecution and rebuilt their lives here.

We’ve published a breakdown of what the DHS memo includes, who could be affected, and what refugees and supporters can do now. The breakdown also includes key links and resources.

If you’re supporting a refugee neighbor, three immediate steps can make a difference:

  • Help them connect with a qualified immigration attorney to discuss their green card application.
  • Support them in creating an emergency preparedness plan.
  • Offer consistent care and reassurance during an unsettling time.

We will continue monitoring developments and share updates as more information becomes available. Staying informed allows us to show up as steady, trustworthy neighbors, especially when policies shift.
